## Why home bedding

Most D2C plays in India chase either fashion or electronics. Home textiles felt like an overlooked category: high repeat purchase potential, meaningful quality differentiation possible, and a customer who was underserved by both fast-fashion mass brands and overpriced import labels.

The insight was simple: Indian households upgrading their living spaces post-pandemic were spending more on the bedroom, but the market was split between cheap bulk options and aspirational brands priced out of reach. There was a real gap in premium-but-accessible.

<PullQuote>
The best category to enter is one where customers already know they want better. They just haven't found it yet.
</PullQuote>

## Starting from zero

We had no inventory, no brand, no supplier relationships, and no customers. What we did have was a clear hypothesis and a 60-day window to test it before committing further capital.

The first two weeks were entirely supplier-side. I drove supplier negotiations across fabric mills and manufacturers, learning more about thread counts, weave types, and minimum order quantities than I expected to. The goal was to find a supply chain that could support premium quality at margins that made the economics work.

<Stat value="40%" label="Gross margin maintained from day one" />

Pricing architecture came next. We built a tiered model — entry, core, and premium SKUs — designed to convert browsers into buyers at the entry level while protecting margin on the hero products. The entry price was set not to maximise margin but to minimise the first-purchase barrier.

## Building the brand

The Ghar Company was positioned as a brand for people who care about how their home feels, not just how it looks. The name itself was a deliberate choice: "Ghar" (home in Hindi) as an anchor to India, "Company" as a signal of seriousness.

Visual identity, packaging, and copy were all built in the first three weeks alongside supplier work. We launched with a tight SKU set: three bedsheet variants, two duvet cover designs. Enough to test preference signals without overcommitting to inventory.

## GTM: online and offline in parallel

We ran two channels simultaneously rather than sequencing them.

**Online:** Instagram and WhatsApp were our primary acquisition channels. Instagram for discovery, WhatsApp for conversion. We built a small content engine around "how to style your bedroom" and "what to look for in bedding" — content that attracted the right customer without being purely product-push. Orders came through a simple Shopify storefront.

**Offline:** We approached two premium home décor stores in Delhi for shelf space on a consignment basis. The offline channel served a dual purpose: direct revenue and social proof. "Available in-store at X" legitimised the brand for online buyers who were on the fence.

<Callout type="insight">
Running online and offline simultaneously created a flywheel: offline presence validated the brand for online shoppers, while online content drove foot traffic to physical touchpoints. Neither channel worked as well alone.
</Callout>

## The numbers

<Stat value="₹2.4L" label="Revenue in 60 days" />
<Stat value="40%" label="Gross margin" />

Revenue of ₹2.4 lakh at 40% gross margins in 60 days. More importantly: we had repeat customers by week six, which validated that the product quality was converting first-time buyers into advocates.

The 60-day constraint was intentional. We gave ourselves one season window to answer the question: can we build a D2C brand with strong unit economics fast? The answer was yes — and the learnings from the supplier network, pricing architecture, and channel strategy are directly applicable to a scaled version of the business.

## What I learned

Building a physical product business, even briefly, fundamentally changed how I think about product development.

In software, you iterate on a feature and ship in hours. In D2C, your iteration cycle is bounded by supplier lead times, inventory commitments, and channel relationships. Speed comes not from removing constraints but from choosing which constraints to bet on early.

The supplier relationships we built in week one determined our margin structure for the rest of the sprint. The pricing decisions we made before launch shaped which customer we attracted. There was no "we'll fix it in the next sprint."

<Callout type="insight">
In physical products, early decisions compound faster and are harder to reverse than in software. The discipline of getting those decisions right — before you have data — is a different muscle than iterative product thinking, and a more valuable one than I expected.
</Callout>

## What's next

The Ghar Company is paused, not closed. The supply chain is in place, the brand has an audience, and the unit economics are proven. The question of when to return to it is a matter of timing and focus — not feasibility.
