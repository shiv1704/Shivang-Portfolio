## Origin

The idea came from a frustration I kept running into at Masters' Union. Every week there was a new announcement — a company "deploying AI at scale," a startup "powered by AI." But when you dug in, the reality was almost always the same: a chatbot, a summarisation tool, maybe a co-pilot that nobody used.

There was no reliable way to understand *how* Indian enterprises were actually adopting AI — not the press release version, but the real version.

<PullQuote>
Everyone is talking about AI adoption. Nobody is measuring it.
</PullQuote>

## The problem

The AI landscape suffers from an information asymmetry. Vendors claim adoption. Buyers don't know what working looks like. Investors are pattern-matching on vibes. And product teams at enterprise companies have no benchmark for whether they're ahead or behind.

Why.AI is being built to close that gap — a platform that tracks, categorises, and benchmarks real AI adoption across Indian enterprises.

## What we're building

{/* TODO: Expand on the specific data model, signal sources, and output formats. */}

The core product is an adoption intelligence layer with three outputs:

1. **Company profiles** — What tools is a company actually using? Where in the workflow? What outcomes have they measured?
2. **Sector benchmarks** — How does adoption in BFSI compare to logistics? What's the median time-to-value in each vertical?
3. **Signal feed** — A curated stream of real adoption signals: job postings, procurement notices, product announcements, case studies — filtered for signal over noise.

## Technical approach

The data collection layer aggregates signals from:
- Job postings (a leading indicator of actual tooling decisions)
- Regulatory filings and annual reports
- Press releases filtered for implementation specifics
- Primary research from the Masters' Union network

## Roadmap

Phase 1 (current): Data collection pipeline + initial company profiles for 50 Indian enterprises.

Phase 2: Sector benchmarking dashboards, subscription access for analysts and investors.

Phase 3: API access for vendors wanting to understand competitive positioning.

## What I'm learning

Building a data product solo is humbling. The hardest part isn't the technology — it's defining what "real adoption" actually means in a way that's measurable and consistent across very different organisations.

<Callout type="insight">
The most important product decision I've made so far: not building a scoring system. Scores flatten nuance. Profiles preserve it.
</Callout>
