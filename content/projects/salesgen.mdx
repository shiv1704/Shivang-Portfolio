## Why I built this

SalesGen started as an experiment: could I take an idea from concept to live prototype in a weekend, using AI-first tooling?

The answer is yes, and the process taught me as much about modern product development as the product itself did.

The problem I was exploring: outbound sales is broken. BDRs spend 60% of their time on research and personalisation that produces emails nobody reads. The remaining 40% is actual selling. AI should be flipping that ratio.

<PullQuote>
The best salespeople I know spend almost no time writing emails. They spend all their time understanding people. SalesGen was built to give everyone that superpower.
</PullQuote>

## What it does

SalesGen is a sales enablement tool that takes an Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) and generates personalised outreach sequences: emails, LinkedIn messages, and call scripts, tailored to each prospect.

Key flows:
1. **ICP Definition:** Define your target customer by role, industry, company size, and pain points.
2. **Prospect Upload:** Upload a list or connect to a data source.
3. **Sequence Generation:** AI generates a multi-touch outreach sequence, personalised per prospect using public information.
4. **Review & Export:** Human review before anything goes out. Export to your CRM or email tool.

The most interesting design challenge was the review step. Early versions just generated and let users send. The problem: users didn't trust it. They wanted to see what the AI was doing and why.

Adding a review layer with visible reasoning ("personalised based on: [Company's Series B announcement]") dramatically increased trust and, counterintuitively, send rates. People felt in control.

<Callout type="insight">
In AI-powered tools, showing the reasoning is often more important than the output quality. Trust precedes use.
</Callout>

## Lessons from building on Lovable

**What worked:**
- Concept to working prototype in ~8 hours
- Easy to iterate on UI without writing CSS
- Good enough for user testing and feedback gathering

**What didn't:**
- Complex state management got messy quickly
- Harder to enforce design system consistency
- Not the right tool for production-grade infrastructure

The right framing: Lovable is excellent for validation. It's not a production stack. I'd use it again for exactly this kind of "does this idea work?" prototype, and then rebuild the core in Next.js if the answer is yes.
